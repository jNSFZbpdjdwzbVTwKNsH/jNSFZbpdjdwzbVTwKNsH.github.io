# Solution to Question 2(c): Choosing δ and Concluding the Bound

## Starting Point

From part (b), we have the inequality:

$$\beta \geq (2^d - 1) e^{-\epsilon \alpha n} (1 - \beta) - (2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+}$$

where $\delta_{N_+} = \delta \cdot \frac{e^{\epsilon N_+} - 1}{e^\epsilon - 1}$ and with probability $\geq 1 - \beta/4$:

$$N_+ \leq \alpha n + c\sqrt{n \log(4/\beta)}$$

## Rearranging to Isolate the Sample Complexity

Rearranging the inequality from part (b):

$$\beta + (2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+} \geq (2^d - 1) e^{-\epsilon \alpha n} (1 - \beta)$$

Dividing both sides by $(1 - \beta)$:

$$\frac{\beta + (2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+}}{1 - \beta} \geq (2^d - 1) e^{-\epsilon \alpha n}$$

Taking logarithms:

$$\log\left(\frac{\beta + (2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+}}{1 - \beta}\right) \geq \log(2^d - 1) - \epsilon \alpha n$$

Therefore:

$$\epsilon \alpha n \geq \log(2^d - 1) + \log(1 - \beta) - \log(\beta + (2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+})$$

This is the inequality stated in your question.

## Key Strategy: Making δ_{N_+} Negligible

To obtain the desired lower bound $n \geq \Omega\left(\frac{d + \log(1/\beta)}{\alpha\epsilon}\right)$, we need:

$$\log(2^d - 1) + \log(1 - \beta) - \log(\beta + (2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+}) = \Omega(d + \log(1/\beta))$$

Since $\log(2^d - 1) = d\log 2 - O(1) = \Theta(d)$ and $-\log \beta = \log(1/\beta)$, the key is to ensure that $(2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+}$ is negligible compared to $\beta$.

**Condition**: If $(2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+} \leq \frac{\beta}{2}$, then:

$$\log(\beta + (2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+}) \leq \log\left(\frac{3\beta}{2}\right) = \log \beta + \log(3/2)$$

## Deriving the Bound

Assuming $(2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+} \leq \frac{\beta}{2}$ and $\beta < 1/2$ (so $1 - \beta > 1/2$):

$$\epsilon \alpha n \geq \log(2^d - 1) + \log(1 - \beta) - \log\left(\frac{3\beta}{2}\right)$$

$$\geq d\log 2 - O(1) - \log(3\beta/2)$$

$$= d\log 2 - \log 3 - \log \beta - O(1)$$

$$= d\log 2 + \log(1/\beta) - O(1)$$

Therefore:

$$n \geq \frac{d\log 2 + \log(1/\beta) - O(1)}{\epsilon \alpha} = \Omega\left(\frac{d + \log(1/\beta)}{\alpha\epsilon}\right)$$

## Explicit Function g(ε, α, d, n)

Now we determine $g$ such that $(2^d - 1) \delta_{N_+} \leq \frac{\beta}{2}$.

With probability $\geq 1 - \beta/4$, we have $N_+ \leq \alpha n + c\sqrt{n \log(4/\beta)}$, so:

$$\delta_{N_+} \leq \delta \cdot \frac{e^{\epsilon(\alpha n + c\sqrt{n \log(4/\beta)})} - 1}{e^\epsilon - 1} \leq \delta \cdot \frac{e^{\epsilon \alpha n} \cdot e^{\epsilon c\sqrt{n \log(4/\beta)}}}{e^\epsilon - 1}$$

We need:

$$(2^d - 1) \cdot \delta \cdot \frac{e^{\epsilon \alpha n} \cdot e^{\epsilon c\sqrt{n \log(4/\beta)}}}{e^\epsilon - 1} \leq \frac{\beta}{2}$$

Solving for $\delta$:

$$\delta \leq \frac{\beta (e^\epsilon - 1)}{2(2^d - 1) \cdot e^{\epsilon \alpha n} \cdot e^{\epsilon c\sqrt{n \log(4/\beta)}}}$$

**Explicit choice**:

$$\boxed{g(\epsilon, \alpha, d, n) = \frac{\beta}{2^{d+1} \cdot e^{\epsilon \alpha n + \epsilon c\sqrt{n \log(4/\beta)}}}}$$

where $c$ is the constant from the concentration bound in part (b).

**Simplified version** (absorbing polynomial factors):

$$\boxed{g(\epsilon, \alpha, d, n) = \frac{\beta \cdot e^{-\epsilon \alpha n}}{2^{d+2} \cdot n}}$$

This ensures that for $\delta \leq g(\epsilon, \alpha, d, n)$ and $\beta < 1/2$, we have:

$$n \geq \Omega\left(\frac{d + \log(1/\beta)}{\alpha\epsilon}\right)$$
